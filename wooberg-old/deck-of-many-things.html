Deck of Many Things

Players must construct a small group deck called the Deck of Many Things.  The Deck of Many Things should contain at least 20 cards. Some guidelines for building it are below.

Cards in the Deck of Many Things should:
  • Have a wide variety of converted mana costs. 5 is a good average.
  • Be roughly balanced among the five colors.
  • Include a variety of card types.
  • Stand on their own.
  • Be powerful, so that players will want to use them.
  • Be fun to use. This is the most important thing.
The Deck of Many Things should not contain:
  • Anything that just produces mana, including lands. No mana is needed to play the cards from the deck.
  • Utility cards. They're not very exciting.
  • Cards that everyone is already using in their decks. It defeats the point.
  • Symmetrical effects. Again, it defeats the point.
  • Cards that work better with a specific color of mana (i.e., cards with activated abilities that cost colored mana). Each card should be usable by any player. (Colored mana in mana costs is irrelevant.)
  • Cards that rely on a specific strategy. Again, each card should be usable by any player.
  • X spells. They don't work. (Kicker and similar abilities are fine if they don't use colored mana.)
  • Anything that won't have the intended effect. You can probably figure it out from the rules. (This really includes everything on this list.)
Gameplay The Deck of Many Things can be part of any multiplayer (or, for that matter, two-player) format. It follows all the normal rules of that format, with the following conditions.

As the game begins, the Deck of Many Things is shuffled and placed face down in the command zone.

During each player's upkeep, before doing anything else, he or she may roll an eight-sided die. (This is a turn-based action and doesn't use the stack.) Whenever a player rolls a 6 or higher, he or she turns the top card of the Deck of Many Things face up. (This is a triggered ability.) He or she may then play that card.

Rather than pay the mana cost of a card from the Deck of Many Things, a player exiles nontoken permanents he or she controls and/or cards from his or her hand and/or graveyard. The number of cards exiled from the player's graveyard, plus twice the number of nontoken permanents he or she exiles, plus twice the number of cards exiled from the player's hand, must be equal to or greater than the card's converted mana cost. (For example, if a card has a converted mana cost of 5, you may pay for it by exiling five cards from your graveyard, three cards from your hand, two cards from your hand and one from your graveyard, or one card from your hand and three from your graveyard. You may also exile any number of nontoken permanents you control instead of that many cards from your hand; so, for example, you could exile two nontoken permanents and one card from your graveyard, or one nontoken permanent, one card from your hand, and one card from your graveyard.)

If a player doesn't play a card he or she has revealed this way, it remains face up in the command zone and is moved off of the deck. During each other player's upkeep, before rolling the die, that player may play that card for the same alternative cost. A player who plays a card this way doesn't roll the die that turn. If no player plays the card, then during the next upkeep of the player who revealed the card, before rolling the die, that player puts the card on the bottom of the Deck of Many Things.

If a card from the Deck of Many Things would be put into a zone other than the stack or battlefield, it's put on the bottom of the deck instead. (This includes instant and sorcery cards immediately after they resolve.)

Variants Obviously, every Deck of Many Things will be different. Some other potential variants include:
  • Change the probability of revealing a card.
  • Change the cost of playing a card from the deck.
  • Require players to bid on cards from the deck. Whenever a card is revealed, the player who revealed it starts the bidding with a bid of any number. In turn order, each player may top the high bid. The bidding ends if the high bid stands. The high bidder plays the card, and the high bid is treated as the card's converted mana cost for the purpose of determining the alternative cost. (A player can't bid higher than the number of cards he or she can exile, counting cards in hand and nontoken permanents twice.)
If you're reading this and you have an idea for a variant, add it to the list!

Pitfalls Strategies that involve self-milling are unbalanced. I'm not sure what to do about this.
